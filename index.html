Resistive Film Based Position Tracking

Extrapolating from the dummy resistor problem, we can use the same fundamental equations to model a slightly more complex problem. Suppose we'd like to track the position of an object along a surface, we can set up a simple tracking system based on a resistive film. Let the base of this object be conductive and held at a constant voltage (e.g. 3.3v). By taking voltage measurements at 3 different points on the film, we can deduce the position of this tracked object along the film. Here, the conductive base of the tracked object is depicted by the rectangular 'inclusion' (H x W: 0.25 x 0.5), and voltage on the resistive film (circle, R: 2.5) is shown by equipotential curves. Three regions (centered around θ: π⁄2 7π⁄6 11π⁄6) act as current sinks and measurement locations.

Experimental method: surface referencing against deformable soft bodies

How does one reference a tool to a deformable soft surface? By applying a matrix of fiducial markers, it is possible to create a high-precision motion tracking system along a deformable surface. Even a low-cost webcam shown here is capable of delivering sub-millimeter precision when tested against ground-truth models.

RE: surface referencing against deformable soft bodies

System in action. Let the wooden block, to which the webcam is mounted, represent our device/tool of interest. The block is of known dimensions and has

1) a set of markers on it for ground-truth comparison, measured by an external camera and
2) a pin set in the bottom of it to mark the center of its contact with the deformable body.

The system determines its contact location by comparing the two frames of reference: camera/tool body and the deformable body.

RE: surface referencing against deformable soft bodies

Interpolating between the fiducial markers on the deformed surface, it is possible to back calculate the contact location w.r.t. the original undisturbed surface. Above: as an example, we may use a simple linear relation to interpolate the poses.

RE: surface referencing against deformable soft bodies

Interestingly, even with a linear interpolation, the system is still accurate to within +/- 1mm of the ground truth. This accuracy can be shown easily when using the system in 'scanning mode', in which the device is pulled away from the contact surface. Here, the actual distance of all surrounding markers w.r.t. the center marker, along the surface, is 45mm.

SMD hand soldering

Typical SMD soldering work done under the microscope. This was a PCB designed and manually soldered for a past project. 0402 package shown here was among the finest-pitched packages soldered for this board, alongside some BGA bluetooth modules. For the best solder joints, my preferred method is to first tin all solder pads; then with a generous amount of tacky flux, tack down components, and solder with a heat gun. Note that this method does not involve solder paste.

Rapid prototyping with medium to high pitch SMDs

This is a great way to rapidly prototype with medium to high pitch surface-mounted devices, without the wait time of a prototype-run PCB. Kapton® tape serves as a surface for gluing down SMT devices as well as an insulation barrier between device and perf-board. Short lengths of magnet wire -- with meltable insulation -- are used to make point-to-point solder connections.
